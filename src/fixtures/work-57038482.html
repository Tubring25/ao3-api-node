Work Text:

Part I

Eventually, Vi was going to get this right.

No, truly, she just needs time.

And patience, probably. She’s actually pretty good at being patient if you ask her. Being in prison for almost 8 years will do that to you.

She’s got roughened up after all the shit she went through, from losing all her family to a car accident and then being wrongfully imprisoned for a crime she didn’t commit.

So yeah, all things considered, one could say she’s been through some shit.

She’s fine now, though.

The good news is, she’s developed an awesome skill in jail, which is basically having the memory of a CIA agent; 26 days since she’s been out of prison, 15 days since she paid the bills, 10 days since she took out the trash and 3 days without showering (she’s still getting used to that, having hot water and being able to shower for longer than 2 minutes).

2796 days since she last saw her siblings alive, 2792 days since she went to prison.

2798 days since she last hugged someone.

Things are going fine, Vi considers.

Right now, she’s just having a hard time learning how to fucking cook a piece of meat because honestly, who thought it was a good idea to let her go out in the world after 7 years in jail just to be her own person?

She’s still learning how to use the shower from her new apartment. Adjusting is hard as fuck, last time she was out was when she was around 15 and apparently, society thought it was a good idea to just, move on without her.

Nice, cool. No hard feelings.

She’s also not using her bed. Or the couch. It’s all still too soft compared to what she was sleeping in a month ago.

The floor from her apartment is just fine, even nicer now that she’s getting used to sleeping with a blanket.

Being cold is shit.

Her neighbors are… nice. Probably. Vi wouldn’t be able to know it anyways since she doesn’t go out.

She’s memorized different ways to escape in case someone decides she’s a threat to the building, probably based on her tattoos or the obvious signs of being an ex-con. So far, nobody has complained about her, which is as nice as it can get for Vi.

Despite her lack of skills when it comes to interacting with people, a local bar called ‘The Last Drop’ still hires her to work all weekends during the night as a server.

Her boss, a guy called Benzo, is okay in her opinion. He pays her enough for Vi to be able to live on her own and pay the bills, so it’s enough for her standards.

Being wrongfully imprisoned also comes with a few perks, one of them being the amount of money she received from the government as a reassurance for, you know, destroying her life. It’s fine, at least she doesn’t have to worry about being in debt.

So far, everything’s been going fine.

3 days since she last went grocery shopping, 8 days since she slept more than 2 hours, 2796 since someone last truly cared for her—

There’s a knock on her door and she feels her body freeze.

Could it be the police? No, she hasn’t done anything wrong, she’s been staying out of trouble.

The old lady from the second floor? Maybe Vi shouldn’t have helped her took out the trash the other day, maybe she should’ve just stayed inside her apartment and not go out and maybe—

It’s a woman. Around Vi’s age, blue hair, and uptight posture. Vi doesn’t think she’s seen her before.

She looks like she’d rather be anywhere else but here and hey, something Vi can relate to.

“Hello, sorry to bother you,” posh accent and a soft voice invade the space, “but it seems like I’ve lost the keys to my apartment.” She explains politely and oh.

So that’s what this is about.

“I didn’t steal them.” She’s quick to clarify before her right to speak is silenced by someone who’s clearly way above Vi’s status.

The woman seems taken aback.

“I… wasn’t accusing you of that. I was just wondering if I could borrow your phone to call a locksmith. My phone has died.” She explains slowly and Vi finally breathes.

“Oh. Uh—sure.” She gestures for her to get in while Vi looks for her phone.

It’s not hard to find it, she only has like 4 valuable objects and one of them is the blanket extended across the living room’s floor.

The woman doesn’t comment on it, thankfully, only picking the phone with a low thank you before making a call.

Distantly, Vi realizes it’s the first time someone’s been inside her apartment. It’s rather pathetic, how out of the loop she is when it comes to people, society.

“Thank you, you saved my life.” The woman says after a few minutes, snapping her back to reality. Vi blinks.

“No problem.” She replies awkwardly.

The woman seems to catch her demeanor and quickly starts motioning to the door.

“I should go now, probably. Thank you again.” Vi opens the door and the woman looks back at her one last time. “I’m Caitlyn, by the way. I live on the apartment from across yours.”

Vi has to physically stop herself from saying something like pink or 516 or fuck off back, slowly managing a low “Vi.” Before closing the door quietly.

She considers it to be her daily dosage of socializing and lays down on the blanket.

She sleeps for 30 minutes before a nightmare wakes her up.

 

 

She has a simple routine, just like in prison.

Of course, there are a few things that change compared to it, like warm food that actually tastes like something or having her own bathroom.

You know, privacy and all that.

But mostly sticks to a strict schedule in order to not think about the things that hurt. It’s something she’s been training for so long now it comes as a second nature.

She’s still shit at cooking, but it takes time, she repeats to herself.

Nightmares come and go without Vi being able to remember what they’re about. She wakes up with her cheeks covered in tears and a cold sensation drowning her body, but she can’t truly know why.

Loud noises and sudden movements make her mind freeze but she tries to ignore it. She sticks to her routine and ignores any weird game her brain might try to pull her into.

Of course, there are a few things she can’t truly leave behind. She was a troubled kid when, you know, she was still a kid. Now, as an adult, that habit hasn’t changed much. She tries hard not to get into fights, to stay out of people’s business, mostly for herself; she’s just getting used to warm showers, she’s not about to lose that again because of a stupid fight.

Except she almost is. There’s a drunk guy at the bar and Vi only manages to take a deep breath for like 10 seconds before her patience is out the window and she’s left with a purple eye. But it’s fine, you should see the other guy.

She half expects Benzo to fire her fight there and then, but he doesn’t, only complains about the drunk dude and tells Vi to take the night off, saying that she deserves it.

Weirdest guy ever. No wonder he runs a bar.

So now she’s back at her apartment, almost 7 in the morning with a black eye that hurts like a bitch and a blanket that it’s still too soft to sleep on.

She sits on the floor doing mostly nothing until there’s a knock on her door.

Here’s the thing, Vi’s an objective person, with an objective perspective of the world. She just punched a guy in public, which means there should be a cop on the other side of the door, right?

Well, the world’s not objective at all, because the person waiting at the door is Caitlyn, her neighbor.

She’s even more confused because Caitlyn takes a double look at her face and frowns, concerned.

“Goodness, are you okay?” she asks startled, and Vi’s brain stops for a second.

“Uh— yeah, peachy. You should see the other guy.” She answers lightly.

She looks down to Caitlyn’s hands holding a box and the blue-haired seems to remember the original reason why she was here in the first place.

“Oh! Right. I brought you something, as a thanks for the other day.” She clarifies, holding the box for Vi to take it.

She hesitates before taking it slowly, suspicious.

“You didn’t have to do that, it’s fine.” Vi doubts.

Caitlyn waves her off.

“Truly, just accept it. I baked enough for a whole family, and they’ll get wasted if they’re at my apartment for too long.”

Vi inspects inside the box to see 6 cupcakes, all elegantly decorated with pink frosting.

“I’ve never had cupcakes before.” She comments lightly, her mouth opening before her brain processes the thought. Caitlyn’s eyes widen.

“Never? Like, ever?” she seems surprised, mostly, but not in a bad way. Vi nods.

“Yep. First time.”

“Oh, well in that case you should let me know what you think about them. I like to bake a lot; it’d be nice to have someone review my baking.” She smiles sheepishly and Vi smiles back shyly.

“Sure, thank you.”

 

 

Turns out, Caitlyn was being modest.

The cupcakes are fucking amazing and Vi has a few seconds to experiment the feeling of jealousy at her talent before she forgets it, too focused on eating them quickly before someone steals it from her. It’s an irrational thought, considering she lives alone. Still, in the back of her mind there’s a voice that whispers for her to hide them under the couch, where no one can take them from her.

The next day, she knocks on Caitlyn’s door to return the box, not without complimenting her baking skills.

She’s about to go back to her apartment when Caitlyn stops her.

“Did you get your eye checked by a doctor?” she asks, concerned.

Vi dismisses her.

“Honestly, it’s fine. Don’t worry about it, I’ve had worse.”

She’s not lying about that, her whole body has more rough scars than a black eye and a little scratch on her eyebrow.

Caitlyn still doesn’t look convinced.

“Would you humor me and let me check it, just in case?” there’s a pleading look and Vi has to look down to ignore the urge to say yes. “My father is a doctor; I’ve got a lot of supplies. Please.”

Vi sighs before nodding.

Caitlyn’s apartment is lot more equipped than Vi’s, it’s the first observation she makes once she’s inside.

All things seem to be on their place just like Caitlyn’s persona; clean and neat. There’s also enough furniture in the living room to equipe Vi’s entire apartment.

She checks her surroundings, taking in the lack of pictures around the walls. She can relate to that.

Caitlyn comes back.

She gestures for Vi to sit on her couch as she looks through a first aid kit.

Vi looks around while she opens the bottle of alcohol and takes a piece of cotton. She hisses quietly when the burning sensation invades the cut above her eye, the blue-haired mutters a low apology before checking her eye.

“Did you put ice on it?”

“No.”

She looks unpleased with that information, “You should, probably. Other than that, I think you’re fine.”

Vi nods, “Nice.”

Caitlyn seems to weight around something before she opens her mouth again, “Is this something that happens a lot?”

“It’s fine,” she dismisses her, “I wasn’t lying when I said I’ve had worse.”

“Oh.” Caitlyn whispers quietly, thoughtful. “Does— is it someone that you know?”

Vi frowns, not following.

“Like, a boyfriend or—”

“Oh, no.” She snorts, “It’s— usually just random assholes on the streets. I have a bad temper, that’s just all.” She waves her hand around.

“Sorry, just wanted to make sure.” Caitlyn explains softly. “You don’t seem like the type to punch people.”

Vi blinks at that, it’s almost comical.

Is she serious?

“Are you blind or something?” she gestures vaguely at her fists and face tattoo.

Caitlyn shakes her head.

“I don’t judge people based on their looks,” she explains, “you were nice to me the other day, it’s all I need to know.”

Vi wants to laugh at how naïve she is, but there’s a weird weight forming in her stomach at the thought of someone perceiving her as nice. She ignores the feeling.

“Well, sorry to break it to you but I am exactly the way I look. Prison will do that to you.” She grimaces, not entirely comfortable about where this is going.

She should probably go back to her apartment before this clearly sheltered person starts screaming for help because a random ex-con just irrupted into her house.

Caitlyn doesn’t bat an eye at that, “Well, that explains the tattoos.” It’s all she says and Vi snorts despite herself.

“Yep, lots of free time.” She comments sourly at the thought. It was way too much free time, just thinking about what could’ve gone different, where Vi could be know if prison hadn’t fucked her up this much.

“How long were you in prison?” Caitlyn voice denotes nothing but curiosity.

“7 years. Almost 8.” She says awkwardly. It’s quite a weird chat to have with her strangely polite neighbor.

“Oh, that’s quite a lot.” Caitlyn reflects softly.

There’s an uncomfortable silence and Vi decides she’s done enough talking for the day, her throat already feels dry and tight. She hasn’t felt the urge to cry in 24 days, she won’t be getting it now.

“Yep. I should— better get going. Thanks for… that.” She ends it awkwardly. Caitlyn nods before getting up and opening her door to let Vi go.

She goes back to her cave. Alone again.

It’s for the better.

 

 

She’s not getting it right at all, actually. The cooking thing.

Her hands are useful for a bunch of shit like punching people or sheltering herself from the angry bats that wardens used to throw at her when she started disobeying, but this? It’s a losing battle.

Apparently, homemade burritos are harder to make than she had originally thought. It’s actually a good thing that she’s only expected to clean the bar and not actually cook because she’d probably be unemployed, and she doesn’t want that.

Of course, her little problem gets magically solved when there’s a gentle knock on her door, Caitlyn waiting on the other side expectantly.

It’s been 12 days since Caitlyn brought her a piece of brownie made by her. Maybe Vi is still surviving purely thanks to Caitlyn’s snacks, the blue-haired seems to have a strange way of coping with stress, cooking large amounts of food even though she lives alone. It’s cool either way, Vi is there.

It’s not specifically relevant information for Vi to keep count on, but there’s a soothing feeling every time she counts to 10-12 days and Caitlyn comes back, with a new snack made for her.

“Quick question.” Caitlyn’s voice snaps her out of her thoughts.

Vi raises her eyebrows amused.

“Hello to you, too.”

She seems to catch herself and there’s a light pink invading her cheeks, “Sorry. Hi, hello. Do you happen to like pasta?”

There’s a vague voice in her head, answering who the fuck doesn’t like pasta? but Vi’s more interested in knowing why Caitlyn wishes to know that.

“Yeah, why?” she frowns.

Caitlyn seems unusually awkward.

“I made pasta and there’s like, a lot of it. I’m bad at proportions. I was wondering if you’d like some of it.”

That is actually the nicest thing someone could offer Vi right now.

Fuck the burritos, she’s in a pasta mood now.

“Sure, I’d like that.”

Caitlyn’s face lightens up at that, clearly pleased. “Good. I’ll—” she mimics back to her apartment, “would you like to come in? Company would be nice.”

Vi falters, “You sure?”

It is one thing to be completely obsessed with your neighbor’s ability to cook but she’s not sure Caitlyn would be delighted to have someone like Vi to eat dinner with. Even Benzo pointed out once, during her lunch break, how she’s sort of an animal when eating.

She doesn’t want to scare her off and end up not getting any more flowery-themed tappers full of brownies.

She’s also just getting used to eating alone without being surrounded by a bunch of strangers who just happen to be in the same shitty place as Vi, eating gray, tasteless food.

The blue-haired nods, “I am.”

She ends up agreeing because the last time someone invited her for dinner was probably way before 2796 days, and Vi can’t even remember how Jericho’s food tastes like or if Powder’s tooth gap was one millimeter or if Vander smelled like wood or flannel—

She resets the count. 0 days starting from now.

 

 

Caitlyn is, in fact, slightly… impressed by Vi’s capacity to slurp her noodles like a fucking vacuum cleaner, but she’s polite enough to just smile and focus on her own plate.

She sits with her back straight and eats with the elegancy of a princess, a napkin resting on her lap.

Across the table, Vi devours her pasta with the delicacy of a bear.

It’s probably the nicest meal she’s ever had in a long time.

At some point she realizes she’s probably being rude by intruding into Caitlyn’s house to eat dinner and not even try to make a conversation.

She looks up from her plate and finds her already staring at Vi with a small smile plastered on her face.

“What?”

Caitlyn seems to falter, searching for the right words.

“Nothing, I mean— I’m honored you’re enjoying it that much.” she comments lightly.

“Is it that obvious?”

“Your face is covered on sauce.”

Vi snorts, “Shit. Sorry.”

Quickly grabbing a napkin, Vi feels her face get hot.

“No, it’s fine.” Caitlyn dismisses her, “It’s quite refreshing, actually. My parents were always strict on table manners, it’s a nice change of pace. You’re clearly, uh—devoted. With food, I mean.”

Vi suppresses a snort. Who the fuck says devoted?

“Yeah, it’s probably a prison thing, although I wasn’t much better as a kid either.”

Caitlyn blinks, taken aback, “Oh, sorry, I wasn’t trying to—”

“It’s cool, don’t worry.” Vi dismisses her quickly.

There’s an awkward silence before Caitlyn opens her mouth again.

“Are you close to your family?”

Vi huffs, “As close as anyone can be, considering they’re 6 feet underground.” She replies lightly.

It clearly doesn’t land as the joke Vi intended it to be, watching Caitlyn’s face morph into an expression of horror.

“Oh my God, I’m so sorry, I shouldn’t—”

Vi snaps out of her mind.

God, what is wrong with her?

“No, sorry. I’m—you’re being nice to me and I’m being a dick. Sorry.” She scratches the back of her neck awkwardly before looking down.

This is the exact reason why she doesn’t socialize. She’s already kissing their little food-dynamic goodbye when Caitlyn interrupts.

“No, it’s completely fine. People usually don’t joke about it, you caught me off-guard.”

She sighs, “Yeah, well, I’m a full-time comedian, that’s me.” This time, Caitlyn does giggle quietly and Vi counts it as a win, maybe she hasn’t completely fucked this up. “But yeah. They’re dead, basically. Parents, siblings, all that stuff.” She motions with her hand awkwardly.

“That’s messed up.” She comments quietly. Vi nods along.

“Mhm, could be worse. It’s fine.”

It is fine, it’s been fine for almost 8 years, it’s not about to not be fine now.

“Do you have any siblings?”

“Nope, I’m an only child.” Caitlyn shakes her head.

Vi hums, “That makes sense.”

The blue-haired narrows her eyes, “What is that supposed to mean?” she asks, suspiciously amused.

“I just knew it. It’s a superpower only people with siblings have.”

Except Vi doesn’t have any siblings anymore.

Caitlyn rolls her eyes, “Oh, I get it, I’m not invited to the cool siblings club because I’m an only child. Real mature.”

“Yep, sorry to break it to you.” She teases back.

She can’t remember the last time she’s been this friendly to someone and said someone was, in return, nice to her.

There’s a pause in the air and they both sigh quietly.

Vi hesitates, “So, I’m not trying to be a dick but it’s getting kind of suspicious how you’re not asking why I’m an ex-con.”

She tries to play it cool even though a part of her is truly curious to know what the fuck is actually going on.

Caitlyn blinks, confused.

“Oh. I just didn’t want to be nosy, I hate it when people try to look into my business. Just figured at some point you’d tell me if you wanted to.” She replies quietly.

“Yeah, but I could’ve murdered someone and be sitting here like nothing happened.”

“Have you murdered someone?”

She stops, “No.”

“Then what did you do?” her curious tone makes it seem like she’s asking Vi about the weather. It’s… confusing.

“Nothing. I was accused of something I didn’t do. Didn’t have the money to pay a good lawyer and ended up in jail, it’s kind of a short story.” She’s skipping all the ugly details, all the things that can’t make it past her throat because it hurts too much to say them out loud.

Caitlyn gasps, “And you were there for 7 years?”

“Yep.”

“Oh, that’s—” Caitlyn frowns, a clear expression of hurt in her face, “I’m so sorry, Vi. That’s terrible.”

She plays it cool, not her first rodeo on the pity stare. Caitlyn’s face does look much more pained, but maybe she’s just too empathetic for her own good. Vi knew a lot of people like that, before 2796 days happened, and she was left alone. Powder used to be too soft, too kind, Vi had a hard time saying no to her more often than not.

“It’s fine. I’m mostly surprised you’re so nice. I was expecting you to freak out when I first told you I went to prison.” She jokes lightly.

Caitlyn snorts, “Right, was it the accent that scared you? Am I too posh?”

“No, it was the stick up your ass.”

Caitlyn lets out a loud laugh and Vi joins her. From her place, she can see the little tooth-gap in between her front teeth, more visible now that she’s laughing.

Caitlyn interrupts first, “I don’t think you give yourself enough credit. You are nice too, prison or not.” And oh, isn’t it quite the lie? Since when do people see her as nice?

She shakes her head, “I’m not nice, you’re definitely projecting.”

Caitlyn doesn’t let go of it. “Your face literally lighted up the other day when I knocked on your door to give you the brownies. And you seem awfully peaceful about the fact that you were wrongfully imprisoned for almost a decade.”

I’m not peaceful at all about it, she thinks internally.

She scoffs, “So what?” hoping Caitlyn won’t catch the way her cheeks are turning pink.

Caitlyn only looks at her for a second before replying, “You are a good person, you won’t be able to change my mind on that.”

There’s a void on Vi’s heart, a full stomach and a feeling of dread at the prospect of not knowing how many days have passed since she last heard that.

She resets the count.

 

 

There’s a lot of things Vi doesn’t know about herself. It’s quite the mystery, all these things she used to remember about her mind, her body, left aside once she was trapped inside a box too small to content her rage. It felt so sudden, the minute she was out in the world, the sky too bright, the rain too loud and the universe too big. It’s overwhelming more often than not, and she’s left wondering how to figure out all these things on her own.

For instance, she sleepwalks. She’s pretty sure she didn’t do that before, you know, all that’s happened, so it is a bit unsettling.

It starts with little things, mundane things, like waking up in the bathroom when she was sure she went to sleep on the living room’s floor. You know, the standard.

Apparently, her sleepwalking self doesn’t have a lot of common sense, hiding her keys under the couch or leaving her cellphone inside the fridge. It’s weird, but she can manage.

She hopes she doesn’t scream or hit things or shit like that, considering her awaken self still needs to be in good terms with her neighbors.

As everything else she tries to keep count of it, the sleepwalking thing. 13 days since it’s started, 9 days since she accidentally hit her head with the bathroom door while sleepwalking, 3 days since she folded her clothes (?). She’s got to be fair; her sleepwalking self is actually useful for a few things.

It’s been 0 days, 0 hours and 4 minutes since she woke up in the middle of one of her sleepwalking sessions to the sound of a glass breaking. She blinks slowly at the sound, not completely sure about why she’s standing in the middle of the kitchen when she’s supposed to be sleeping.

“Fuck.”

This is going to be a problem. But hey, when has something in Vi’s life been anything other than that?

 

 

Caitlyn finds new recipes and Vi has the chance to try them all out. She’s been out of prison for 86 days now, met Caitlyn 60 days ago. It’s oddly comforting to keep count on that.

She’s not close to her family, that’s the latest thing Caitlyn’s told her about herself. Politician mother with high standards Caitlyn could never meet, ended up leaving to become a journalist and doesn’t talk on a daily basis to her mom, who’s mostly disappointed she didn’t follow her path to get into politics.

“Honestly, I’m surprised you didn’t point it out the first time we met. Everybody else in this building has at some point stopped me to ask me if I was Cassandra Kiramman’s daughter.” Caitlyn comments absent-mindedly. They’re in her apartment again, eating freshly baked croissants (Vi’s still amazed at her talent to bake such tasteful things when she can’t even boil an egg without making a disaster).

She snorts, “You’re forgetting the fact that not so long ago I was living inside a concrete cell.” She jokes.

Caitlyn rolls her eyes, “I can’t even imagine how you manage to joke so easily about that.”

Vi shrugs, “Told you, full-time comedian.” The blue-haired doesn’t bat an eye at the response and Vi sighs. “It’s just easier. To joke about it, I mean.” She ends up saying quietly. Caitlyn hums.

“I get it. For what it’s worth, I’m here, if you need to talk about it in a non-joking way.” Caitlyn adds softly and Vi can only nod at that, her throat closing around nothing.

 

 

The sleepwalking comes and goes, the nightmares not so much.

Her mind can barely get the picture of what is happening during her nightmares, the only proof that they’re there is the fact that Vi keeps waking up at ungodly hours with her face covered in tears and her heart running too fast. There’s a sense of loss, of a void inside her and she can’t explain why.

In contrast with her sleepwalking, which is mostly quietly wandering through her apartment, her nightmares start soft and escalate until they’re loud enough to suffocate her. Vi knows she screams sometimes when she’s having them, even if she can’t remember it, her throat is sore enough to be proof of it.

And Caitlyn, too. If her being at her door at 2 A.M. means anything.

“You were screaming. I thought something had happened.” Is the response she gets. Cool, now she’s bothering her neighbors too.

“Yeah, sorry. Nightmare.” Her eyes are probably red from rubbing them angrily.

“Oh.”

“Sorry for waking you up.”

Caitlyn blinks, a hurt expression on her face, “No, it’s fine. Are you okay?”

“Yep. I’ll probably just stay awake, don’t wanna wake up anybody else.”

“Would you like me to stay with you?” Caitlyn hesitates before asking.

Except for the key incident, Caitlyn has never been inside Vi’s apartment. It’s even rich to call it an apartment, only having a cheap couch and a few things of her own. The blanket it’s still splattered on the floor, even if Vi can’t sleep for more than a few hours each night.

Caitlyn hums, lying next to her, visibly more comfortable and relaxed than Vi. She doesn’t exactly remember how they ended up in this scenario, sometimes Caitlyn just tags along for support. It’s quite helpful.

“Weird how?” she asks curiously.

“Like I’m— I don’t know. I don’t like it.” It’s vague. And explains absolutely nothing.

“Like you’re going to fall into it?” she provides.

How does she always know?

“Sort of.” She sighs, “Also makes me hurt my back.”

Caitlyn hums.

She can’t stand it anymore. As if her body had its own will, she gets up quickly and gets as far away from the bed as possible, like it’s burning her bones.

“Sorry, maybe next time.”

The blue-haired nods happily, looking at her watch.

“We made it into 49 minutes, that’s new record.”

Vi snorts.

“Yay for me.”

 

 

She learns to detect the things that trigger her mind the wrong way. She makes a list and keeps it close, in case new things become part of those signs.

Dark spaces, small places, sharp noises.

Gray light, canes, cold.

Car horns, ambulance sirens, needles, stuffed toys (not all of them, just… the ones that are bunny-shaped), children screaming, orange juice, TV static, thunderstorms, elevators, foggy days-

It’s… a lot.

And it seems endless.

Her therapist takes a quick look at it and nods, pleased.

The real problem comes with the next assignment.

“Now make a list of things that are soothing for you. That make you feel comfortable and at ease.”

It’s a completely different story. According to her therapist there are no rules on what makes her feel one way or the other, Vi just needs to feel and that’s it. No guidelines.

It’s a lot more difficult without any parameter. She starts with stupid things.

Plants, for example. They are pretty stupid things if she’s objective. But they’re very green and very calm. So, she scribbles it down.

Chocolate cookies too. And banana bread.

Also having a routine feels very much safe-y. Schedules and plans and knowing what is going to happen, when is going to happen. Calendars are useful for that. She has a strict schedule on everything that happens from Mondays to Sundays, work hours and when to eat, how many times a week she should water her plants. 10 minutes each day for shower time and at least 2 hours a week to spend at Caitlyn’s.

Caitlyn’s apartment, Caitlyn’s kitchen, Caitlyn’s vanilla cookies.

Caitlyn.

 

 

So, there is something Vi needs to keep count on from now on.

But it’s…

How the fuck does she transcribe it to words on paper?

The whole thing with trying to sleep on an actual bed will have to wait because now all of her attention is focused on another bigger, important task.

She kissed Caitlyn, and Caitlyn kissed her back. Or Caitlyn kissed her and Vi kissed back? It’s kind of a blur. Dizzy, even.

Yes, that’s how she felt, dizzy. In a good way, a very good way. They were supposed to be baking lemon cookies at Caitlyn’s kitchen when it happened. Vi can’t remember who leaned in first, maybe it was mutual, who knows?

All she knows is that Caitlyn’s mouth tasted like sugar and lemon juice which is exactly what Vi has pictured in her head when imagining that moment. The aftermath of it, being able to see her turn a light shade of pink and giggle nervously like a teenager was probably Vi’s favorite part to replay in her head.

Internally, she thinks she hasn’t existed this immensely in her whole life.

And it’s only been 0 days, 2 hours and 29 minutes. Should she wait 21 hours and 31 minutes to write it down and start keeping count of it? Maybe.

It’s honestly hard to spiral down the panic aisle since Caitlyn is lying next to her on the blanket. Not too much space to start having an existential crisis.

“Was it okay that I kissed you?” Caitlyn whispers.

“Yeah.” That’s what she answers because it was fine. It’s probably the first thing in Vi’s life that has actually been fine and not some sort of not fine, but you’ll get used to it.

Even better, if Caitlyn is still here, there’s a chance she might get to kiss her again. Or get kissed. Both options are fine, she’ll take them both. One at a time or simultaneously.

Point is, it’s fine.

There’s an arm passing around her middle and a warm presence settling in the crook of her neck. Silk, blue hair invades her vision as the sleepiness inside of her starts crawling back. Her mind turns off.

She’ll get time to panic later.

 

 

 There’s no panic later on, just a bunch of chaos on Vi’s little mental note.

2 days since she last kissed Caitlyn.

1 day since she last kissed Caitlyn.

2 hours since she last kissed Caitlyn.

30 minutes since she last

3 minutes since she

After that, she stops writing it down. It’s a waste of paper.

 

 

Sometimes, it’s the other way around and she learns the things that trigger Caitlyn. Not like, literally trigger, but more of no-no things that should be avoided.

Her mother is probably at the top of the list (imaginary list, since Caitlyn insists that Vi doesn’t need to keep such a strict leash on those things). Her dad doesn’t count, Caitlyn is quite fond of him despite her mother’s clear influence.

High school is a sore topic, childhood too. Caitlyn tells Vi about it either way. Says it makes her feel better.

Carrot cake is her only true enemy, the one that should be avoided at any costs and Vi do needs to check on. Allergies are shit.

 

 

“Ready?”

“Hm, let’s do it.”

It’s like an old cowboy movie. The ones she used to watch on TV with Powder when they were little.

Vi (unwillingly) vs. bed. That’s it.

Caitlyn offered herself as the referee (cuddling companion) for the task.

Her legs won’t cooperate, watching the enemy from afar.

“Or I could crash on your couch again.” She suggests.

Caitlyn doesn’t bat an eye.

“Vi, you haven’t even tried it yet.”

“I don’t like those sheets.” She tries again, petulant.

“You bought those sheets.”

An undignified noise comes out of her mouth, close to a whine. Caitlyn puts her thinking face on.

“Listen, just try to lay down for a few minutes, if you start feeling weird, we’ll move onto the couch. I’ll scratch your head until you fall asleep.”

She hesitates. The couch is still her favorite place to fall asleep, beds are overrated.

“Okay, deal.”

The whole process ends up being more boring than she had expected but she does get under the covers, and she has the bonus of Caitlyn laying down next to her with a hand running through her scalp.

Her eyes start to get heavy without notice and she has the vague feeling that tomorrow she’ll need to scribble down a few things.

0 days, 0 hours and 0 minutes.
